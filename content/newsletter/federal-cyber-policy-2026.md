Policy rarely makes headlines the way a splashy data breach does — but if you're planning a
career in security, the rules being written in Washington right now may shape your job more than
any single incident.

## Secure-by-design is becoming the default

Regulators are increasingly expecting software to ship secure *by default*, rather than leaving
users to harden it themselves. That shift is creating demand for engineers who understand threat
modeling, memory-safe languages, and secure SDLC practices — skills you can start building today.

> The takeaway: security is moving "left," into design and development. Roles that blend software
> engineering with security fundamentals are growing fastest.

## Incident reporting changes the day-to-day

New reporting timelines mean organizations must detect, triage, and disclose incidents faster than
ever. For students, this translates into rising demand in:

- **Detection & response** — SOC analysts, threat hunters, and IR specialists.
- **Governance, risk & compliance (GRC)** — translating regulation into practice.
- **Security communications** — explaining technical risk to non-technical leaders.

## What you can do now

1. Follow a policy tracker or a newsletter like this one to stay current.
2. Practice writing clearly — the ability to explain risk is a superpower.
3. Join us for a workshop where we map real regulations to real technical controls.

Policy isn't separate from the technical work — it's the frame around it. Understanding both makes
you the person teams reach for.
